# Fruit Drink Co. — Pomegranate Juice Turnaround

**Source:** Wharton Casebook, 2025  
**Type:** Profitability · **Industry:** Consumer (CPG) · **Difficulty:** 2/5  
**Format:** Candidate-led · **Stretch area:** Brainstorming

---

## Prompt

*[Read this to the candidate. This is a candidate-led case — minimize hand-holding and let them drive.]*

We have been hired by a large CPG client. One of their product lines is a pomegranate drink. They've observed that they are losing market share to the competition, and their profitability is lower compared to the competition. We have been asked to help them turn around the situation.

---

## Clarifying Questions

*A strong candidate would ask 2-3 of the following before structuring their approach:*

**Q:** What is the client's objective?  
**A:** Understand why market share and profitability are lower than the competition, and how to turn the situation around.

**Q:** What is the client's business model?  
**A:** The client manufactures the drink and sells through third-party retailers and distributors.

**Q:** What geography are we operating in?  
**A:** US-based client, with sales across the US.

**Q:** How do we compare to the competition today?  
**A:** Client market share is 20% vs. 36% for the main competitor. Client profit margin is −8% vs. +10% for the competitor. Profits have been in the red for the last two years. Other players exist, but little data is available on them.

---

## Framework

This is a profitability problem with a competitive overlay: the client is losing on both share *and* margin, and the margin is negative while the competitor's is positive. Because the loss is recent (two years) and firm-specific rather than industry-wide, the strongest structure anchors on the **cost and revenue structure of the product unit** and compares each lever directly against the competitor — rather than a generic market-first tree.

**Strong hypothesis to open with:** *"My hypothesis is that there's been a change in the client's revenue or cost structure for making the pomegranate juice. I'd want to compare our fixed and variable costs against the competitor's. Do we have any data suggesting our costs could be higher?"*

## Question 1: Cost structure observations

> *We have information on the fixed and variable costs per product versus our main competitor. What are your observations here?*

### Exhibit A: Per-unit cost analysis — Client vs. Competitor

![Exhibit A: Per-unit cost analysis](/case-library/CA-003/exhibit_a.png)

**Note:** Price of a 12×8 oz juice pack is USD 15 for both brands. Exclude fixed costs from this analysis.

### Math

<div class="case-calculation">

| Component | Client | Competitor | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pomegranate | 11.5 | 9.0 | +2.5 |
| Water | 0.8 | 0.8 | 0.0 |
| Other ingredients | 0.2 | 0.2 | 0.0 |
| Manufacturing | 0.8 | 1.5 | −0.7 |
| Packaging | 1.2 | 0.5 | +0.7 |
| Distribution | 1.4 | 1.4 | 0.0 |
| Sales & Marketing | 0.3 | 0.1 | +0.2 |
| **Total cost / unit** | **16.2** | **13.5** | **+2.7** |

**Per-unit profit** = price − total variable cost:
- Client: 15 − 16.2 = **−1.2** per unit → consistent with the −8% margin
- Competitor: 15 − 13.5 = **+1.5** per unit → consistent with the +10% margin

</div>

**Sample answer:** Three components drive the client's disadvantage — **pomegranate (11.5 vs. 9.0)**, **packaging (1.2 vs. 0.5)**, and **sales cost (0.3 vs. 0.1)**. The client is actually *cheaper* on manufacturing, so it isn't worse on everything. Net, the client loses ~1.2 per unit while the competitor earns ~1.5.

## Question 2: Why are the costs higher?

*This is a brainstorming question — push the candidate for structured hypotheses, at least 4 ideas (top 5% reach 7-8).*

> *What are your hypotheses for each of the cost levers being higher than the competitor's?*

| Hypothesis | What the data reveals |
|---|---|
| **Pomegranate** cost is higher — higher pomegranate content, or higher procurement price | The competitor has an exclusive venture with an irrigation-equipment company: they supply equipment to farmers in exchange for the right to buy directly. The client buys from wholesalers, paying more. Best case, the client could only reduce this to **11.3**. |
| **Packaging** cost is higher — type, cost, or amount of packaging | The competitor uses tetrapaks; the client uses plastic bottles. Switching is an option, but would only match the competitor's **0.5**. |
| **Sales & marketing** cost is higher — different channels or higher spend per channel | (Open — candidate should explore channel mix and spend efficiency.) |

<div style="margin-top: 1.5rem;"></div>

**Advanced insight:** Even after reducing pomegranate and packaging costs, the client is still ~USD 0.3/unit unprofitable. There is little incentive to chase market share — the client should instead look to **raise price**. (This sets up Question 3.)

---

## Question 3: Recommendation for the CEO

> *The CEO is about to walk into the room. What are your recommendations for her?*

**Recommendation:** With the existing cost structure, Fruit Drink Co. should **raise price** and justify the increase to retain or grow share — rather than relying on cost cuts alone.

**Supporting reasoning:**
1. Even after exhausting every realistic cost-reduction option, the client remains unprofitable at the unit level (pomegranate floors at ~11.3, packaging at best matches 0.5 — still a ~0.3/unit loss).
2. A higher price can turn the unit profitable and position the brand as premium — reinforced by taste improvements and quality signaling through packaging and advertising.
3. With margin negative, chasing additional share at the current price only deepens losses; price is the higher-leverage move.

**Risks / considerations:**
- Customers may be unwilling to pay more for the juice packs (price elasticity).
- Packaging or marketing changes could alienate existing customers.

**Next steps:**
- Run a price-elasticity exercise with customers.
- Conduct a product clinic to test willingness to buy the repackaged, repositioned product.

## Interviewer Notes

**Great candidates will:**
- Open with a clear hypothesis and a structure tailored to a cost/competitor comparison, not a generic profitability tree.
- On the exhibit, immediately isolate the three driver components *and* notice manufacturing runs the other way.
- In Question 2, brainstorm in a structured way and push past three ideas — strong candidates give 4+ hypotheses (procurement model, packaging type, channel mix); top candidates reach 7-8 and contextualize each as a short story.
- Reach the counterintuitive insight that cost cuts alone don't restore profitability, so price is the real lever.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Treating it as pure cost-cutting and missing that the unit stays unprofitable even after cuts.
- Recommending a share grab while margin is negative.
- Ignoring that the competitor's pomegranate cost advantage is structural (exclusive direct-sourcing), not just better negotiation.

**Signal to push on:** After the candidate proposes cost reductions, ask *"Does that fix the problem?"* — the great answer recognizes the residual per-unit loss and pivots to pricing.
