# Electro Chargers

**Source:** Wharton Consulting Club Casebook, 2024-25  
**Type:** Market Entry · **Industry:** Automotive / EV Infrastructure · **Difficulty:** 4/5  
**Format:** Candidate-led · **Stretch area:** Structuring

---

## Prompt

Your client, Electro Chargers, is a manufacturer of electric vehicle battery swap stations that are used to house batteries for swappable electric vehicles. Rather than charging vehicles at charging stations, customers come to the stations when their batteries get depleted (<10% charge) and swap their vehicle's batteries with batteries from the station.

Electro Chargers is considering entering a new city to set up its chargers. Rebecca, the CEO, wants our support in deciding which city to enter. She also wants our support in estimating the amount of fixed investment the firm would require until 2030 to set up a battery swapping network in the city.

---

## Clarifying Questions

*Some examples of clarifying questions a candidate could consider asking. There is no need to ask all of them — pick the ones that matter most for sharpening your understanding before structuring.*

**Q:** What is the **objective**?  
**A:** To shortlist the city to enter based on potential number of EVs in the city (demand for the chargers) and estimate the fixed investment required until 2030.

**Q:** What is the **geography** of operations?  
**A:** Electro Chargers is a US-based company and already has operations in Boston. They have shortlisted 5 cities: New York, Washington DC, Seattle, Miami, and Atlanta.

**Q:** What is the **business model**?  
**A:** Swap stations are an alternative to traditional charging stations or home charging setups. The time taken for customers is similar to refueling at a gas station.

**Q:** What **technology constraints** should we know about?  
**A:** These batteries are only suitable for 4-wheeler passenger cars with swappable batteries. Batteries are interoperable and can be used with any swappable EV.

## Framework

This is a market entry case with both quantitative (which city, what investment) and qualitative (capabilities, entry strategy) components. The candidate should structure the problem to address both the *city selection* decision and the *investment sizing* in parallel, while also considering execution capabilities.

## Question 1: Which city should the client enter?

> *We have some information for the shortlisted cities. Based on this information, which city should the client enter and set up its operations?*

### Exhibit A: Shortlisted city characteristics

![Exhibit A: City characteristics](/case-library/CA-002/exhibit_a.png)

**Note:** Cost of gas (per gallon) is the same across all cities. Swappable EVs provide a mileage equivalent to ~21 miles/gallon for gas-powered vehicles.

### Step 1: Quantify demand

Calculate potential swappable EVs by 2030 for each city using the formula: **# of EVs × Swappable EV % × (1 + growth rate)**

<div class="case-calculation">

| City | Calculation | Potential swappable EVs 2030 | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| New York | 60,000 × 8% × (1 + 100%) | **9,600** | ✅ Top contender |
| Washington DC | 20,000 × 5% × (1 + 80%) | 1,800 | ❌ Eliminated (low demand) |
| Seattle | 30,000 × 12% × (1 + 100%) | **7,200** | ✅ Top contender |
| Miami | 30,000 × 5% × (1 + 150%) | **3,750** | ✅ Top contender |
| Atlanta | 15,000 × 6% × (1 + 80%) | 1,620 | ❌ Eliminated (low demand) |

</div>

**Three cities advance:** New York (9,600), Seattle (7,200), and Miami (3,750). DC and Atlanta are eliminated due to low demand.

### Step 2: Apply qualitative filters

Compare the three remaining cities across qualitative factors:

<div class="case-calculation">

| Factor | New York | Seattle | Miami |
|---|---|---|---|
| Competing firms | 5 | 4 | 4 |
| Policy support | Medium | **High** | Medium |
| Gas mileage (incentive to switch) | 1/15 (strong) | 1/24 (weak) | **1/10 (strongest)** |

</div>

**Insight:** Gas mileage is the swing factor. The *lower* the miles/gallon for gas-powered vehicles, the *stronger* the incentive to switch to EVs. Seattle's high gas efficiency (1/24) means gas users have less reason to switch — eliminating Seattle despite its policy support and high demand.

### Step 3: Final ranking

1. **New York** — highest absolute demand (9,600) + strong switch incentive (1/15 gas mileage)
2. **Miami** — strongest switch incentive (1/10) + manageable demand (3,750)
3. Seattle — high demand but weakest switch case

**Recommendation: enter New York.**

## Question 2: How many stations are needed and what's the fixed investment?

> *How many stations would Electro Chargers need to set up in New York, and what's the required fixed investment until 2030?*

### Exhibit B: Comments from Mr. Albert, COO of Electro Chargers

![Exhibit B: COO Comments](/case-library/CA-002/exhibit_b.png)

**Key data from the COO:**
- For every 100 swappable EVs in a city, ~10 swap stations are needed (i.e., 1 station serves ~10 EVs)
- Electro Chargers targets 10% market share by 2030

### Exhibit C: Fixed investment cost structure

![Exhibit C: Fixed investment requirements](/case-library/CA-002/exhibit_c.png)

### Math

**Step 1: Calculate number of stations needed**

<div class="case-calculation">

**Number of stations** = # of EVs × (stations / EV) × market share

= 9,600 × (10 / 100) × 10%

= 9,600 × 0.10 × 0.10

= **96 stations**

</div>

**Sanity check:** ~100 stations across NYC feels reasonable — not so few that coverage is sparse, not so many that the network is overbuilt. If the candidate's answer comes out at 9.6 or 960, they should catch the order-of-magnitude error.

**Step 2: Calculate total fixed investment (using ~100 stations as a working number)**

<div class="case-calculation">

| Cost line | Per-unit | Multiplier | Total ($M) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Station setup | $55,000 | × 100 stations | $5.5M |
| Rent (one year, 2030 onward) | $5,000/yr | × 100 stations | $0.5M |
| SG&A for operations (2025-2030) | — | flat | $4.0M |
| Overheads (maintenance, contracts) (2025-2030) | — | flat | $2.0M |
| **Total fixed investment** | | | **$12.0M** |

</div>

**Answer:** ~96 stations, ~$12M total fixed investment to 2030.

## Question 3: What other considerations should they think about?

> *What are some other considerations for Electro Chargers before entering the new city?*

A strong candidate should structure brainstorming into 2 categories and provide at least 4 ideas (top 5% provide 7-8):

**A. Business and technical considerations**
- **Capital for investment** — can the firm fund $12M+ of upfront fixed cost?
- **City knowledge** — product preferences, cultural fit, administrative/geographic/economic differences
- **Competitive advantages** — what's our differentiation vs. the 5 NYC competitors?
- **Customer awareness** — willingness to switch from gas vehicles or other charging methods
- **Brand awareness and reputation** — Electro Chargers is established in Boston, but unknown in NYC
- **Human capital** — operations team, leadership, local hires
- **Technical capabilities** — app, software, integration with EV manufacturers

**B. Entry strategy considerations**
- **Market entry timing** — go now or wait for higher EV penetration?
- **Piloting options** — start with 5-10 stations and scale, or full launch?
- **Centralized vs. decentralized control** — manage from Boston HQ or set up local ops team?
- **Other risks** — flooding (Miami/NYC coastal), regulatory, supply chain

**Best practice:** Present horizontally with a 10-second top-down overview before diving into specifics. E.g., *"I'd like to think about two kinds of considerations — business-related and entry-strategy-related. On the business side, we'd need to consider funding, brand awareness, customer education, and human capital. On the entry side, we'd look at timing, piloting, and operating model."*

## Interviewer Notes

**Great candidates will:**
- Quickly identify that **demand (potential EV count)** is the primary screening filter for city selection
- Catch the **gas mileage gotcha** — Seattle looks attractive on demand and policy, but the strong incumbent gas economy weakens the switching incentive
- Sanity-check the 96-station math: 9.6 or 960 are obvious order-of-magnitude errors
- Differentiate **business considerations** from **entry-strategy considerations** in brainstorming rather than producing a flat list
- Recommend a phased entry approach (pilot → scale) given the $12M investment risk

**Common pitfalls:**
- Picking Seattle based on policy support without examining the gas-mileage switch incentive
- Treating the 9,600 calculation as the answer to "how many stations" (off by ~100×)
- Forgetting to multiply by 10% market share in the station calculation
- Producing brainstorming that's just a flat list of risks (e.g., "competition, money, regulation") without structuring

**Signal to push on:** Ask the candidate, *"What would change your recommendation if Seattle's gas mileage suddenly worsened to 1/12?"* — strong candidates can pivot quickly and articulate that Seattle's qualitative strengths (policy, demand) would now outweigh New York's edge.
