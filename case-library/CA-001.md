# Fruit Drink Co.

**Source:** Wharton Consulting Club Casebook, 2024-25  
**Type:** Profitability · **Industry:** Consumer Packaged Goods · **Difficulty:** 2/5  
**Format:** Candidate-led · **Stretch area:** Brainstorming

---

## Prompt

We have been hired by a large CPG client. One of their product lines is a pomegranate drink. They have observed that they are losing market share to the competition, and their profitability is lower compared to the competition. We have been asked to help them turn around the situation.

---

## Clarifying Questions

*Some examples of clarifying questions a candidate could consider asking. There is no need to ask all of them — pick the ones that matter most for sharpening your understanding before structuring.*

**Q:** What is the client's **objective**?  
**A:** Understand why market share and profitability are lower compared to competition, and how to turn the situation around.

**Q:** What is the client's **business model**?  
**A:** The client is a manufacturer of the drink and uses third-party retailers and distributors.

**Q:** What is the **geography** of the client's operations?  
**A:** The client is US-based with sales across the entire US region.

**Q:** What do we know about the **competitive landscape**?  
**A:** Our market share is 20% vs. 36% for our main competitor. Our profit margin is -8% compared to 10% for the competitor. We have been in the red for the last two years. Other players exist but we don't have much data on them.

---

## Framework

This is a classic profitability-and-market-share case. The candidate should propose a structure that allows them to diagnose *why* the client is underperforming on both axes simultaneously, then identify levers to close the gap.

I'd hypothesize the issue is on the cost side and would want to compare our fixed and variable costs to the competitor's."*

## Case Math

**Question:** What is the client's per-unit profit/loss, and the competitor's?

<div class="case-calculation">

**Client:**
- Price: $15.00
- Total cost: $11.5 + $0.8 + $0.2 + $0.8 + $1.2 + $1.4 + $0.3 = **$16.20**
- Per-unit profit: $15.00 − $16.20 = **−$1.20/unit** (loss)

**Competitor:**
- Price: $15.00
- Total cost: $9.0 + $0.8 + $0.2 + $1.5 + $0.5 + $1.4 + $0.1 = **$13.50**
- Per-unit profit: $15.00 − $13.50 = **+$1.50/unit**

**Per-unit gap:** $2.70/unit (and the client is losing $1.20 on every unit sold)

</div>

<div class="case-calculation">

**Where the cost gap comes from:**
- Pomegranate: $11.5 vs $9.0 → **$2.5 gap** (the dominant driver)
- Packaging: $1.2 vs $0.5 → $0.7 gap
- Sales & Marketing: $0.3 vs $0.1 → $0.2 gap

</div>

## Brainstorming

*"What are your hypotheses for each of the cost levers being higher than the competitor?"*

**1. Pomegranate cost (highest impact: $2.5 gap)**
- *Hypothesis:* Higher pomegranate content per unit, or higher procurement price
- *Reality:* Competitor has an exclusive venture with an irrigation equipment company — they provide equipment to farmers in exchange for direct purchase rights. The client buys from wholesalers (with markup).
- *Best-case reduction:* Client can only get pomegranate cost down to $11.30 (a $0.20 improvement, not enough to close the gap)

**2. Packaging cost ($0.7 gap)**
- *Hypothesis:* Different packaging type, more material, or higher per-unit cost
- *Reality:* Competitor uses tetrapaks; client uses plastic bottles. Switching to tetrapaks is feasible.
- *Best-case reduction:* Match competitor's $0.5 (a $0.7 improvement)

**3. Sales & marketing cost ($0.2 gap)**
- *Hypothesis:* Different channels or higher per-channel spend
- *Less material to the recommendation; the gap is small*

**Advanced insight:** Even if the client fully reduces pomegranate cost ($0.20 saving) and matches competitor packaging ($0.70 saving), they still face a residual loss of approximately **$0.30 per unit**. Cost reduction alone cannot make this business profitable. **The client must look at increasing price.**

## Interviewer Notes

**Great candidates will:**
- Open with a sharp hypothesis tying "only losing money in last 2 years" to a firm-specific (not industry-wide) issue
- Quickly recognize Exhibit A shows the client is unprofitable per unit *before* even doing the math
- Propose at least 4 brainstorming hypotheses for the cost gap (top 5% propose 7-8)
- Arrive at the counterintuitive "raise price, don't chase share" conclusion rather than defaulting to cost-cutting
- Acknowledge the structural nature of competitor's pomegranate cost advantage

**Common pitfalls:**
- Defaulting to "we need to cut costs to match the competitor" without checking whether that's even sufficient
- Spending too long on the market/industry branch when the data points to cost
- Recommending market share recapture without first fixing unit economics
- Missing the "exclude fixed costs" note on the exhibit and trying to incorporate them

**Signal to push on:** Ask the candidate, *"What if the competitor also drops their price to match yours?"* — strong candidates recognize this triggers the title of Case 7 ("Race to the Bottom") and articulate why brand differentiation matters.
